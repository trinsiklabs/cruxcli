BELIEF ID: POL-088
BELIEF: National sovereignty is the fundamental principle of international order; no external authority should override a nation's self-governance.
SYSTEMS: Nat, RP, TC, NC, FNS, Mon, RL
PRESUPPOSITION: The nation-state is the largest unit at which self-governance is meaningful; supranational authority is unaccountable.
ASSESSABLE AS: "No international organization or foreign power should be able to override decisions made by a country's own government."
UNIQUE: No — broadly nationalist and sovereigntist.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Sovereignty absolutism enables genocide, environmental destruction, and aggression — some international constraints are necessary to prevent catastrophe. Systems: GI, SLP, SD, DS.
RESEARCH: The Westphalian system (1648) established sovereignty as the organizing principle of international relations. The UN Charter (1945) both affirms sovereignty (Art. 2.1) and limits it (Chapter VII allows intervention for threats to peace). The Responsibility to Protect (R2P, 2005) explicitly limits sovereignty when states commit atrocities. Empirically, sovereignty absolutism enabled the Rwandan genocide (1994, 800,000 dead) — the international community refused to intervene citing sovereignty. Counter: humanitarian interventions have mixed records (Libya 2011 removed a dictator but produced a failed state). Brexit (2016) was partly a sovereignty reassertion against EU authority. The tension between sovereignty and international cooperation is genuinely irresolvable.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — national sovereignty is valuable for self-determination but absolute sovereignty enables atrocity and blocks collective action on global challenges.

BELIEF: International institutions and agreements are necessary to solve problems that no single nation can address alone.
SYSTEMS: GI, SLP, SD, DS, CL (trade agreements)

The evidence supports international institutions for specific technical problems; their track record on complex political problems is weaker.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — international institutions have clear successes (ozone, trade, disease) but significant failures on complex political issues; the need for coordination is undeniable, the optimal form is debated.

BELIEF ID: POL-090
BELIEF: Military strength and willingness to use force are essential to maintaining peace and national security.
SYSTEMS: NC, TC, Nat, FNS, Mon, RP (some)
PRESUPPOSITION: The world is dangerous; weakness invites aggression; peace is a product of strength, not good intentions.
ASSESSABLE AS: "A strong military and the willingness to use it are the best guarantees of peace and security."
UNIQUE: No — broadly realist and right-of-center.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Military spending diverts resources from human needs; arms races create the very insecurity they claim to prevent; diplomacy and economic interdependence are superior peace strategies. Systems: RL, LP, AS, ACom, LL.
RESEARCH: "Si vis pacem, para bellum" (if you want peace, prepare for war) has ancient roots. NATO's Cold War deterrence arguably prevented WWIII — nuclear deterrence maintained the "Long Peace" (Gaddis, 1986). However, global military spending ($2.2 trillion in 2022, SIPRI) dwarfs humanitarian aid ($35 billion). The security dilemma (Herz, 1950): one nation's defensive preparations look offensive to others, fueling arms races. Costa Rica abolished its military in 1948 and has been stable and prosperous since, contradicting the necessity claim. Japan's post-WWII pacifism coincided with extraordinary prosperity. The U.S. spends more on military than the next 10 countries combined, yet has been at war for 225 of its 248 years — suggesting military strength and peace are not straightforwardly correlated.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — deterrence has some empirical support; the claim that military strength is THE best guarantee of peace is not supported by cross-national comparison.

BELIEF ID: POL-091
BELIEF: Military interventionism and regime change in other countries can be justified to promote democracy and human rights.
SYSTEMS: NC, SLP (humanitarian intervention variant)
PRESUPPOSITION: Some regimes are so oppressive that the international community has a responsibility to act; sovereignty does not protect tyrants.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Sometimes it is right for powerful countries to intervene militarily to stop oppression or promote democracy in other countries."
UNIQUE: No — neoconservative and liberal interventionist.

The humanitarian intervention in Kosovo (1999) arguably prevented genocide but bypassed the UN Security Council. The R2P framework (2005) attempts to limit intervention to atrocity prevention, but this principle was misused in Libya.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal (against) — military intervention has an overwhelmingly poor track record for democracy promotion; the rare successes required conditions (unconditional surrender, massive investment) that are unlikely to be replicated.

BELIEF ID: POL-092
BELIEF: Military intervention in other countries' affairs is almost never justified and usually makes things worse.
SYSTEMS: RL, AC, LP, AS, ACom, TC (paleoconservative), LL

The "first, do no harm" principle in medicine may apply: if interventions usually fail, the bar for action should be very high. The ICISS report (2001) tried to resolve this with the Responsibility to Protect framework — intervention only as a last resort, only for mass atrocities, only with reasonable prospect of success.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — the intervention track record is poor enough to support a strong presumption against it; absolute non-interventionism cannot account for genuine atrocity prevention.

BELIEF ID: POL-093
BELIEF: Imperialism and colonialism are the primary causes of global inequality and their effects must be actively remedied.
SYSTEMS: MC, DS, SLP, LP, ACom, AS, GI (some), PC
PRESUPPOSITION: Wealthy nations became wealthy by extracting resources and labor from colonized peoples; the current world order perpetuates this extraction.

COUNTER-BELIEF: Colonialism ended decades ago; countries that were colonized have had sufficient time to develop; persistent poverty reflects domestic governance failures, not colonial legacy. Systems: CL, RL, TC, NC, RP.

Singapore, Hong Kong, and South Korea achieved rapid development despite colonial histories. The "primary causes" claim may overstate colonial legacy relative to geography, culture, and post-independence policy choices — though these factors interact with colonial legacies.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — colonialism's causal role in shaping global inequality is well-established; whether it remains the PRIMARY cause decades later is debated.

BELIEF: Nations have a moral obligation to accept refugees fleeing persecution and war.
SYSTEMS: SLP, GI, SD, DS, LL

COUNTER-BELIEF: Nations' first obligation is to their own citizens; unlimited refugee acceptance strains public services, depresses wages, and threatens social cohesion. Systems: Nat, RP, FNS, TC.

CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — the moral and legal case for refugee protection is well-established; the practical limits of absorption and political sustainability are also real.

BELIEF ID: POL-095
BELIEF: A nation's first obligation is to its own citizens; foreign aid and refugee acceptance should be secondary to domestic needs.
SYSTEMS: Nat, RP, TC, FNS
PRESUPPOSITION: The social contract is between a government and its own people; diluting resources and attention to help outsiders betrays that contract.

UNIQUE: No — broadly nationalist and populist-right.
COUNTER-BELIEF: In an interconnected world, neglecting foreign crises (pandemics, terrorism, climate change) eventually harms domestic citizens too; pure nationalism is both morally narrow and strategically short-sighted. Systems: GI, SLP, SD, LL.

Foreign aid effectiveness is debated: Easterly ("The White Man's Burden," 2006) argues most aid is wasted; Deaton ("The Great Escape," 2013) finds aid has had limited impact on development. Counter: the Marshall Plan ($13 billion, ~$150 billion in 2023 dollars) successfully rebuilt Europe and created U.S. trading partners and allies — the most successful foreign aid program in history. The COVID pandemic demonstrated that neglecting global health creates domestic consequences. OECD countries spend 0.3% of GNI on foreign aid (far below the 0.7% target), suggesting the trade-off is rarely as stark as framed.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — governments do prioritize citizens (empirical fact); whether they SHOULD do so exclusively is normative; the evidence suggests that some international engagement serves domestic interests.
