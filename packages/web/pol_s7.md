BELIEF ID: POL-098
BELIEF: Environmental protection, including aggressive action on climate change, should be a top policy priority even at economic cost.
SYSTEMS: SLP, SD, DS, GI, ACom (some), LL, GP
PRESUPPOSITION: Ecological collapse threatens civilization itself; short-term economic costs are trivial compared to long-term existential risks.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Protecting the environment should be a top priority even if it means slower economic growth or higher costs."
UNIQUE: No — broadly progressive and internationalist.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Environmental regulation is often exaggerated in urgency and used to justify government expansion; economic growth provides the resources needed to address environmental problems. Systems: RL, AC, TC (some), NC.
RESEARCH: IPCC AR6 (2023): 1.5°C warming threshold likely breached by 2030; 2°C by mid-century on current trajectory. Climate damages estimated at 10-23% of global GDP by 2100 (Burke et al., 2015). The Stern Review (2006) estimated climate mitigation costs at 1% of GDP vs. damages of 5-20% — a massive return on investment. Counter: Nordhaus (Nobel 2018) argues for gradual carbon pricing rather than aggressive action, estimating optimal warming at ~3.5°C. Lomborg ("Cool It," 2007) argues resources are better spent on adaptation than mitigation. However, the IRA (2022) showed that climate investment can drive economic growth — clean energy investment hit $1.7 trillion globally in 2023 (IEA). The economic cost of climate ACTION is consistently lower than the economic cost of inaction.
CONFIDENCE: irrefutable — climate change is real and human-caused (97%+ scientific consensus); the optimal policy response is debated but the case for significant action is overwhelming.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-099
BELIEF: Environmental regulation is often a pretext for government expansion and economic control; market solutions are superior.
SYSTEMS: RL, AC, some TC, some NC
PRESUPPOSITION: Property rights and market incentives solve environmental problems better than bureaucratic regulation; environmentalism is often anti-growth ideology.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Environmental problems are better solved through markets and property rights than through government regulation."
UNIQUE: No — market-environmentalist position.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Markets created environmental destruction in the first place — externalities (pollution, emissions) are the textbook case of market failure; without regulation, companies will destroy the commons. Systems: SLP, SD, DS, MC, GP.
RESEARCH: The U.S. SO2 cap-and-trade program (1990) reduced sulfur emissions 36% at one-quarter the projected cost — a clear market-mechanism success. Carbon pricing exists in 73 jurisdictions covering 23% of global emissions (World Bank, 2023). Coase theorem (1960) shows private bargaining can solve externalities — but only with low transaction costs and clear property rights, conditions rarely met for environmental problems. Counter: no company has ever voluntarily stopped polluting without regulatory pressure. The unregulated U.S. (pre-1970) had rivers catching fire (Cuyahoga, 1969), toxic smog, and rampant industrial pollution. The Clean Air Act (1970) produced $32 in benefits for every $1 in costs (EPA, 2011). Property rights cannot address global commons problems (atmosphere, oceans) because ownership is undefined.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — market mechanisms (cap-and-trade, carbon pricing) are effective tools WITHIN a regulatory framework; purely market-based solutions without regulation have no successful environmental precedent.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-100
BELIEF: Economic growth and environmental protection are fundamentally compatible through technology and green innovation.
SYSTEMS: SLP, CL (some), SD, GI, GP (some)
PRESUPPOSITION: Human ingenuity can decouple growth from environmental destruction; "degrowth" is neither necessary nor desirable.
ASSESSABLE AS: "With the right technology and innovation, we can have both a growing economy and a healthy environment."
UNIQUE: No — green-growth position across several systems.
COUNTER-BELIEF: "Decoupling" is a fantasy — no country has achieved absolute decoupling of GDP growth from resource consumption and emissions at the scale needed. Systems: MC, ACom, DS (some), GP (degrowth wing).
RESEARCH: Relative decoupling (reducing emissions per unit of GDP) has occurred in many countries — EU emissions fell 24% while GDP grew 60% (1990-2022). However, absolute global emissions continue to rise. The "rebound effect" (Jevons paradox): efficiency gains lead to more consumption, offsetting savings. Denmark and Sweden have achieved near-zero-emission electricity grids while growing GDP. Counter: Hickel & Kallis (2020) argue that no country has achieved absolute decoupling of GDP from material footprint. Global resource extraction hit 100 billion tonnes in 2019 (UNEP) and is rising. The green-growth thesis works for energy but may not work for materials, land use, and biodiversity. The most optimistic models still require massive investment ($4-6 trillion annually, IEA) and policy intervention.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — partial decoupling is demonstrated; full decoupling at the scale needed to address the climate crisis is not yet proven and may not be possible through growth alone.
```

BELIEF ID: POL-101
BELIEF: Capitalism's growth imperative is fundamentally incompatible with ecological sustainability; the system must change.
SYSTEMS: MC, ACom, AS, DS (some), LP (some), GP (degrowth wing)
PRESUPPOSITION: An economic system that requires infinite growth on a finite planet is suicidal; only post-capitalist organization can achieve sustainability.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Our current economic system's need for constant growth is fundamentally incompatible with a healthy planet."
UNIQUE: No — ecosocialist and left-environmentalist.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Capitalism's incentive structure is the only system that has produced the innovation needed to address environmental challenges; solar, wind, and battery technology exist because of market incentives. Systems: CL, RL, NC, AC.
RESEARCH: Global material extraction doubled from 1990 to 2019 while GDP roughly tripled — suggesting GDP growth drives resource extraction. Raworth ("Doughnut Economics," 2017) argues for an economic model that meets social needs within planetary boundaries. Jackson ("Prosperity Without Growth," 2009, revised 2017) argues growth dependency is structurally embedded in capitalism. Counter: the energy transition IS happening through capitalist incentives — solar costs fell 89% in a decade (IRENA, 2022); EVs went from <1% to 18% of new car sales globally (2023, IEA). The question is whether capitalist innovation can move fast enough. Global CO2 emissions need to halve by 2030 (IPCC) — this has never been achieved under any economic system.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — the tension between growth and sustainability is real; whether capitalism can be reformed fast enough or must be replaced is genuinely unresolved.

BELIEF ID: POL-102
BELIEF: Technological progress is generally beneficial and should be accelerated, not restricted by excessive precaution.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC, SLP (techno-optimist wing), GI, TL
PRESUPPOSITION: Innovation has consistently improved human life; precautionary principle applied too broadly stifles progress.
ASSESSABLE AS: "We should generally embrace new technologies and not let excessive fear of risks slow down progress."
UNIQUE: No — broadly technologically optimistic.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Unregulated technology has produced nuclear weapons, social media addiction, surveillance states, climate change, and may produce uncontrollable AI; precaution is wisdom, not fear. Systems: TC, GP, ACom, some SLP.
RESEARCH: Technological progress has doubled life expectancy, connected the globe, and lifted billions from poverty. The Green Revolution (1960s-70s) saved an estimated 1 billion lives through agricultural technology (Borlaug). Counter: social media's mental health effects (Haidt, "The Anxious Generation," 2024 — correlating smartphone adoption with teen depression spike); AI existential risk (Hinton, Bengio, and others warning of catastrophic potential); nuclear weapons' ongoing existential threat. The precautionary principle (Wingspread Statement, 1998) is standard in EU regulation but criticized for blocking beneficial technologies. The key question is not technology vs. no technology, but the pace and governance of technological deployment.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — technology has been overwhelmingly beneficial historically; the current pace of AI development raises genuinely novel risks that prior techno-optimism may not adequately address.

BELIEF ID: POL-103
BELIEF: Technology in the hands of corporations and governments is primarily a tool of surveillance and control.
SYSTEMS: AS, ACom, LL, LP (some), RP (some)
PRESUPPOSITION: Power uses technology to expand its reach; without radical democratization of tech, it will be used against ordinary people.
ASSESSABLE AS: "New technologies are primarily being used by governments and corporations to monitor and control ordinary people."
UNIQUE: No — anti-authoritarian position across spectrum.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Technology is a tool that can be used for liberation or oppression; the pessimistic framing ignores how technology has empowered individuals (smartphones, social media enabling protests, encryption, open-source software). Systems: CL, SLP, TL, GI.
RESEARCH: China's social credit system and mass surveillance of Uyghurs demonstrate technology-enabled authoritarianism. Zuboff ("The Age of Surveillance Capitalism," 2019) documented corporate data extraction as a new form of economic exploitation. Snowden (2013) revealed mass government surveillance. Counter: technology also enabled the Arab Spring, Hong Kong protests, #MeToo, and global whistleblowing. Encryption tools (Signal, Tor) protect dissidents and journalists. Open-source software and decentralized technologies (blockchain, federated social media) represent counter-movements. The internet's overall effect on political freedom is debated — it enables both mobilization and surveillance, both free expression and misinformation.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — both surveillance and liberation uses of technology are well-documented; the "primarily" characterization depends on context — authoritarian regimes weaponize technology; democratic societies show a more balanced picture.
