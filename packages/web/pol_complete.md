## MASTER BELIEF CATALOG

### SECTION 1: INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS AND LIBERTY

```
BELIEF ID: POL-001
BELIEF: Every person possesses inherent natural rights that exist prior to and independent of government.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC, LL
PRESUPPOSITION: Human beings have intrinsic moral worth that is not granted by any institution; rights are discovered, not created.
ASSESSABLE AS: "People have fundamental rights that no government created and no government can legitimately take away."
UNIQUE: No — shared across classical liberal tradition and libertarian variants.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Rights are social constructs created and enforced by political communities; without a state to enforce them, "natural rights" are fictions (Bentham: "nonsense upon stilts"). Systems: MC, FNS, Mon, TG.
RESEARCH: The natural rights framework traces to Locke (1689) and was operationalized in the U.S. Declaration of Independence and French Declaration of the Rights of Man. Bentham's critique (1843) called natural rights "rhetorical nonsense." Modern debate: Amartya Sen's "Elements of a Theory of Human Rights" (2004) argues rights can be grounded without metaphysics. The UN Universal Declaration (1948) adopted natural-rights language but with positivist enforcement. Cross-culturally, many societies ground dignity in relational/communal rather than individual terms (Donnelly, "Universal Human Rights in Theory and Practice," 2013).
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — philosophically contested; no empirical test can determine whether rights are "discovered" or "invented," but the practical effects of rights-based governance are measurable.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-002
BELIEF: Individual liberty is the highest political value and should be maximized in all policy decisions.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC
PRESUPPOSITION: Individuals are the best judges of their own interests; freedom produces better outcomes than control.
ASSESSABLE AS: "When designing laws and policies, expanding personal freedom should be the top priority."
UNIQUE: No — shared across right-liberal and libertarian systems, though CL allows more state function than RL/AC.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Liberty is one value among several (equality, solidarity, security, order) and must be balanced against them; maximizing liberty alone produces a society that serves the strong at the expense of the weak. Systems: SD, SLP, TC, Com.
RESEARCH: Isaiah Berlin's "Two Concepts of Liberty" (1958) distinguishes positive and negative liberty, showing that "maximizing freedom" depends on which concept you use. The Human Freedom Index (Cato/Fraser Institute) correlates economic freedom with GDP per capita (r≈0.65) but the happiest countries (Nordic states) score high on both freedom AND state provision (World Happiness Report 2023). Amartya Sen's capability approach argues that substantive freedom requires material preconditions, not just absence of interference.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — whether liberty should be the HIGHEST value is normative; empirical data shows liberty is one component of well-being, not the sole determinant.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-003
BELIEF: Rights are primarily negative — they protect individuals FROM interference rather than guaranteeing provision of goods.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC, TC, NC
PRESUPPOSITION: Freedom means absence of coercion; positive entitlements require coercing others to provide them.
ASSESSABLE AS: "A right to healthcare means the government can't prevent you from seeking care — not that someone else must pay for it."
UNIQUE: No — distinguishes the right-of-center spectrum from the left-of-center spectrum.
COUNTER-BELIEF: The negative/positive rights distinction is artificial; all rights require state action and funding to enforce (even property rights require courts, police, registries). Systems: SLP, DS, SD, MC, LL.
RESEARCH: Henry Shue ("Basic Rights," 1980) argues all rights require positive duties — even the right to physical security requires police, courts, and prisons (taxpayer-funded). Stephen Holmes & Cass Sunstein ("The Cost of Rights," 1999) demonstrated empirically that enforcing property rights and contracts costs the U.S. government hundreds of billions annually, undermining the negative/positive dichotomy. The ICESCR (1966) codifies economic/social rights in international law, ratified by 171 states (the U.S. signed but never ratified).
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — the philosophical distinction is coherent, but the empirical claim that negative rights are "costless" is demonstrably false.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-004
BELIEF: Rights are both negative and positive — society has an obligation to ensure people can actually exercise their freedoms.
SYSTEMS: SLP, DS, SD, MC, LL, AS, ACom, LP
PRESUPPOSITION: Formal freedom without material capacity is hollow; real liberty requires resources.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Having the legal right to do something is meaningless if you lack the resources to actually do it."
UNIQUE: No — defines much of the left-of-center spectrum.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Positive "rights" are actually claims on others' labor and resources, making them fundamentally different from rights that merely require non-interference. Enforcing positive rights requires coercion. Systems: CL, RL, AC.
RESEARCH: T.H. Marshall ("Citizenship and Social Class," 1950) traced the historical expansion from civil to political to social rights. Empirically, countries with stronger social rights (Scandinavia, Germany) show higher social mobility than the U.S. (Corak, "Income Inequality, Equality of Opportunity, and Intergenerational Mobility," 2013). The "Great Gatsby Curve" shows negative correlation between inequality and mobility across OECD nations. However, Nordic models also rely on high levels of social trust and cultural homogeneity that may not transfer.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — evidence consistently shows that material conditions affect the ability to exercise formal rights, though the optimal policy mechanism is debated.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-005
BELIEF: Freedom of speech should be nearly absolute, including speech that offends, disturbs, or challenges authority.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC, LL, TL
PRESUPPOSITION: The remedy for bad speech is more speech; suppression is more dangerous than any utterance.
ASSESSABLE AS: "People should be free to say things that most of society finds offensive or wrong."
UNIQUE: No — shared across libertarian and classical liberal traditions.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Absolute speech protection ignores power asymmetries; hate speech causes measurable harm to targeted groups and can enable violence (as in Rwanda's Radio Milles Collines). Systems: SLP, MC, DS, some SD.
RESEARCH: The U.S. First Amendment represents the strongest speech-protection model; most European democracies restrict hate speech without collapsing into tyranny. Empirical analysis: Richard Delgado & Jean Stefancic ("Must We Defend Nazis?", 2018) document harms of hate speech. Counter-evidence: Jonathan Rauch ("Kindly Inquisitors," 2013) argues speech restrictions historically target dissenters more than bigots. Cross-national data shows no clear correlation between hate speech laws and reduced prejudice (Heinze, "Hate Speech and Democratic Citizenship," 2016). Germany's post-war speech restrictions are cited as successful; France's similar laws have not prevented rising far-right sentiment.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — both strong-protection and restriction models have been implemented in functioning democracies; empirical evidence on optimal policy is mixed.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-006
BELIEF: Speech that causes demonstrable harm to marginalized groups can and should be regulated.
SYSTEMS: SLP, MC, DS, some SD
PRESUPPOSITION: Power imbalances mean some speech functions as oppression rather than expression; protecting the vulnerable outweighs abstract speech rights.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Society is justified in restricting speech that targets and harms vulnerable groups."
UNIQUE: No — progressive and left positions broadly.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Defining "harm" is inherently political and will be controlled by whoever holds power; hate speech laws are routinely used against the minorities they claim to protect. Systems: CL, RL, AC, LL.
RESEARCH: Mari Matsuda et al. ("Words That Wound," 1993) provided the theoretical framework. Empirically, the European Court of Human Rights has upheld hate speech restrictions in dozens of cases. However, analysis of UK hate speech prosecutions shows disproportionate enforcement against racial minorities and LGBT people posting on social media (Amnesty International UK, 2018). Canada's Section 13 (hate speech provision) was repealed in 2013 after being used primarily against marginal figures rather than powerful bigots. Mueller & Schwarz (2021) found anti-refugee social media posts correlated with anti-refugee violence in Germany.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — the harm is documented but optimal regulatory approaches remain contested; enforcement patterns raise legitimate concerns about who gets targeted.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-007
BELIEF: The right to private property is foundational to all other rights and freedoms.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC, TC, NC, Mon (qualified)
PRESUPPOSITION: Without secure property, individuals cannot be independent agents; property enables self-determination.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Private property rights are the foundation that makes all other freedoms possible."
UNIQUE: No — broadly right-of-center, though monarchism qualifies it with aristocratic obligations.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Property rights are one right among many, not foundational; historically, property rights were used to justify slavery, exclude women from civic life, and concentrate power. Systems: MC, AS, ACom, DS, LL.
RESEARCH: Hernando de Soto ("The Mystery of Capital," 2000) showed that clear property rights correlate with economic development in developing nations — formalized property in Peru increased investment by 300%. Acemoglu & Robinson ("Why Nations Fail," 2012) found inclusive property institutions predict prosperity. Counter: Piketty ("Capital in the Twenty-First Century," 2014) demonstrated that returns on capital exceed economic growth (r>g), creating self-reinforcing wealth concentration. The Enclosure Acts (England, 1750-1850) showed property formalization can dispossess the poor while enriching landlords. Indigenous land dispossession in the Americas illustrates the violence underlying "foundational" property claims.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — property security correlates with economic development, but the claim that it is FOUNDATIONAL to ALL other rights is a philosophical position, not an empirical finding.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-008
BELIEF: Private property in productive resources (land, factories, capital) is a form of theft from the commons.
SYSTEMS: MC, ACom, AS
PRESUPPOSITION: Natural resources and socially-created productive capacity belong to everyone; private appropriation is illegitimate.
ASSESSABLE AS: "When one person owns a factory or farmland, they are effectively taking something that belongs to everyone."
UNIQUE: No — shared across anti-capitalist left.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Property is created through labor and investment, not appropriated from a commons; the "commons" without property rights produces the tragedy of the commons. Systems: CL, RL, AC, TC.
RESEARCH: Proudhon's "What Is Property?" (1840) originated the "property is theft" formulation. Marx's theory of primitive accumulation documented the historical violence of enclosure. Empirically, collective ownership experiments have mixed results: Israeli kibbutzim showed high productivity initially but most privatized by 2000s (Abramitzky, "The Limits of Equality," 2018). Elinor Ostrom (Nobel Prize 2009, "Governing the Commons") demonstrated that commons CAN be managed collectively without privatization under specific institutional conditions — contradicting both the "tragedy of the commons" and the "all property is theft" positions.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — the normative claim about legitimacy cannot be empirically tested, but the practical effects of different property regimes are extensively studied.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-009
BELIEF: Individuals should have absolute sovereignty over their own bodies, including drug use, sexual practice, and medical decisions.
SYSTEMS: RL, AC, LL, CL (mostly), AS, ACom, TL
PRESUPPOSITION: Self-ownership is the most fundamental property right; no one has a superior claim to your body.
ASSESSABLE AS: "What a person does with their own body is entirely their business, even if others disapprove."
UNIQUE: No — shared across libertarian spectrum and some liberal positions.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Individual bodily choices have social consequences (addiction burdens healthcare, drug markets fund violence, anti-vaccination endangers others); the "sovereign individual" is a myth in an interdependent society. Systems: TC, TG, NC, SD (public health).
RESEARCH: Portugal's drug decriminalization (2001) reduced overdose deaths by 80% and HIV infections among users by 95% by 2015 (EMCDDA data). Oregon's 2020 decriminalization showed more mixed results — increased usage without adequate treatment infrastructure. Prohibition-era U.S. (1920-1933) demonstrated that banning alcohol increased organized crime and had minimal effect on consumption. Seatbelt and helmet laws, which override bodily sovereignty, are estimated to save 15,000+ U.S. lives annually (NHTSA). COVID-era vaccine mandates showed tension between bodily sovereignty and public health — mandates accelerated vaccination but fueled political backlash.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — evidence strongly supports decriminalization over prohibition for drugs; the absolute sovereignty claim is harder to maintain for choices with clear externalities (vaccination, driving safety).
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-010
BELIEF: Individual rights must be balanced against community well-being and social cohesion.
SYSTEMS: TC, SD, SLP, NC, TG, Mon, Nat, RP, Com
PRESUPPOSITION: Humans are social creatures whose flourishing depends on healthy communities; unchecked individualism is corrosive.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Sometimes individual rights need to be limited to protect the well-being of the broader community."
UNIQUE: No — very widely held across communitarian spectrum.
COUNTER-BELIEF: "Balancing" rights against community interests is how rights get eliminated; rights exist precisely to protect individuals FROM community preferences. Systems: RL, AC, LL.
RESEARCH: Amitai Etzioni ("The Spirit of Community," 1993) articulated the communitarian position. Robert Putnam ("Bowling Alone," 2000) documented declining social capital and its negative effects. Empirically, the most successful societies combine strong individual rights WITH strong communal obligations (Nordic model). East Asian societies (Japan, South Korea, Singapore) prioritize community cohesion and achieve high well-being scores, though with trade-offs in individual expression. COVID-19 responses illustrated the tension starkly: collective-oriented societies (East Asia, New Zealand) had lower death rates initially; individualistic societies (U.S., UK) had higher deaths but faster economic recovery.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — the need for SOME balance is nearly universally acknowledged; the disagreement is over where to draw the line.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-011
BELIEF: The right to bear arms is essential to maintaining freedom against tyranny.
SYSTEMS: RL, AC, CL (historically), RP
PRESUPPOSITION: An armed populace is the ultimate check on government power; disarmament precedes oppression.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Ordinary citizens being armed is an important safeguard against government tyranny."
UNIQUE: No — primarily right-libertarian and populist right.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Modern military technology makes citizen militias irrelevant against government tyranny; the practical effect of widespread gun ownership is increased gun violence, not increased freedom. Systems: SLP, SD, DS, GI.
RESEARCH: The U.S. has both the highest civilian gun ownership (120.5 per 100 people) and the highest gun death rate among wealthy nations (12.2 per 100K in 2022, CDC). Cross-national comparison: Australia's 1996 buyback reduced gun homicides by 42% and gun suicides by 57% (Chapman et al., 2016). The "tyranny prevention" thesis lacks historical support — the most tyrannical 20th-century regimes (Nazi Germany, Soviet Union) arose despite armed populations. Conversely, many unarmed democracies (UK, Japan, Australia) have never experienced tyranny. However, armed resistance DID play roles in some liberation movements (American Revolution, French Resistance).
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — the tyranny-prevention claim lacks strong empirical support; the correlation between gun availability and gun violence is strong_signal.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-012
BELIEF: Privacy from government surveillance is a fundamental right that should rarely be compromised.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC, LL, SLP, TL
PRESUPPOSITION: Government power tends toward abuse; surveillance chills free thought and association.
ASSESSABLE AS: "The government should almost never be allowed to monitor citizens' communications or activities."
UNIQUE: No — broad coalition across liberal and libertarian positions.

China's social credit system demonstrates surveillance-state potential. However, targeted surveillance has prevented terrorist attacks — the UK's MI5 estimates disrupting 30+ plots since 2017. Studies show most citizens accept some surveillance for security (Pew 2015: 56% of Americans find NSA phone tracking "acceptable").
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — surveillance demonstrably chills behavior; the debate is about where to draw the line, not whether privacy matters.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-013
BELIEF: Security concerns can justify significant surveillance and restriction of civil liberties.
SYSTEMS: NC, FNS, TG, Mon, Nat (some), RP (some)
PRESUPPOSITION: External and internal threats are real and dangerous; liberty means nothing if the society is destroyed.
ASSESSABLE AS: "In the face of serious threats, it is reasonable for the government to expand surveillance and limit certain freedoms."

Japanese-American internment (1942) is the canonical example of security overreach — considered a national shame, formally apologized for in 1988. Israel's ongoing state of emergency (since 1948) shows how "temporary" security measures persist indefinitely. However, the UK's prevention of 30+ terror plots (MI5 data) and disruption of extremist networks demonstrates real security benefits from surveillance capabilities.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — both the security benefits and the civil liberties costs of expanded surveillance are well-documented; the trade-off is real, not imaginary.

### SECTION 2: THE STATE AND GOVERNANCE

```
BELIEF ID: POL-014
BELIEF: The legitimate purpose of government is solely to protect individual rights (life, liberty, property).
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, AC (minimal or none)
PRESUPPOSITION: Government is a necessary evil at best; its only justification is preventing worse evils (violence, theft, fraud).
ASSESSABLE AS: "The only legitimate job of government is protecting people from violence, theft, and fraud."
UNIQUE: No — the minimal-state position shared across the classical liberal spectrum.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Government has evolved to serve many legitimate purposes — infrastructure, education, public health, environmental protection — that go far beyond rights protection and that markets cannot provide efficiently. Systems: SLP, SD, DS, TC, NC, Com.
RESEARCH: Robert Nozick ("Anarchy, State, and Utopia," 1974) provided the philosophical framework for the minimal state. Empirically, the most prosperous societies all have governments far exceeding the minimal-state model — no wealthy nation limits government to rights protection alone. The 19th-century "night-watchman state" era in the U.S. and UK produced massive inequality, child labor, and environmental destruction that prompted progressive-era reforms. Public goods theory (Samuelson, 1954) demonstrates market failure in providing non-excludable goods. The Heritage Foundation's Economic Freedom Index shows top-ranking countries (Singapore, New Zealand, Switzerland) all maintain substantial public sectors beyond mere rights enforcement.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — philosophically coherent but no successful modern example of a minimal state exists; all prosperous nations have extensive government functions.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-015
BELIEF: Government has a positive duty to promote the general welfare beyond mere rights protection.
SYSTEMS: SLP, SD, DS, TC (qualified), NC, Mon, TG, LP, Com
PRESUPPOSITION: Society is more than a collection of individuals; collective action through government can achieve what individuals cannot.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Government should actively work to improve people's lives, not just protect them from crime."
UNIQUE: No — very widely held, though with vastly different conceptions of what "general welfare" means.
COUNTER-BELIEF: "Promoting the general welfare" is an infinitely expandable mandate that inevitably leads to government overreach, bureaucratic bloat, and loss of individual freedom. Systems: RL, AC.
RESEARCH: The expansion of government functions in OECD countries (from ~10% of GDP in 1900 to ~35-55% today) correlates with massive improvements in life expectancy, literacy, infant mortality, and poverty reduction. The Nordic model (government spending 45-55% of GDP) consistently ranks highest on happiness, social mobility, and human development indices (UNDP HDI, 2023). Counter-evidence: government spending beyond ~40% of GDP shows diminishing returns in some economic growth studies (Afonso et al., 2005), and public choice theory (Buchanan & Tullock, 1962) documents how welfare-promoting mandates get captured by special interests.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — the correlation between expanded government functions and improved well-being is robust across countries and time periods, though the optimal scope is debated.
```

```
BELIEF ID: POL-016
BELIEF: The state is inherently an instrument of class oppression and must be abolished.
SYSTEMS: MC (end-state), ACom, AS
PRESUPPOSITION: The state exists to enforce the interests of the ruling class; it cannot be reformed, only destroyed and replaced.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Government fundamentally exists to protect the interests of the powerful at the expense of everyone else."
UNIQUE: No — shared across revolutionary left and anarchist traditions.
COUNTER-BELIEF: The state is a contested institution that can serve any class or interest depending on who controls it; abolishing it doesn't eliminate power — it just makes power unaccountable. Systems: SD, SLP, TC, CL.
RESEARCH: Marx's historical analysis of the state as superstructure has partial empirical support: Gilens & Page (2014) found that U.S. policy outcomes correlate with elite preferences (r≈0.78) and are unrelated to average citizen preferences when elites disagree. However, social democratic states (Scandinavia) have demonstrably served working-class interests through the state. All historical attempts to abolish the state (Russian Revolution, Spanish Civil War anarchist zones, Cambodian Year Zero) either rebuilt states quickly or collapsed into chaos. Ostrom's work on polycentric governance offers alternatives to both statism and abolition.
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — the class-bias claim has empirical support; the abolition prescription has no successful historical precedent.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-017
BELIEF: The state is inherently an instrument of coercion and should be abolished in favor of voluntary association.
SYSTEMS: AC, ACom, AS
PRESUPPOSITION: All state authority rests on violence; voluntary cooperation can handle all legitimate social functions.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Every function currently performed by government could be better handled by voluntary cooperation."
UNIQUE: Partially — the all-anarchism position; distinguishable from MC which envisions a transitional state.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Voluntary cooperation cannot solve collective action problems at scale (defense, pollution, pandemic response); some coercion is necessary to prevent free-riding. Systems: CL, SLP, SD, TC, NC.
RESEARCH: The Zapatista autonomous municipalities (Chiapas, Mexico, 1994-present) demonstrate partial anarchist governance, though they exist within a state framework. Rojava (northeastern Syria, 2012-present) implements democratic confederalism with mixed results. Historical anarchist experiments: Ukraine's Free Territory (Makhnovshchina, 1918-1921) functioned briefly before being crushed by the Red Army; Spanish anarchist Catalonia (1936-1939) showed both remarkable social organization and internal violence. Experimental economics research (public goods games) consistently shows voluntary cooperation decays without enforcement mechanisms (Fehr & Gächter, 2000).
CONFIDENCE: hypothesis — small-scale voluntary cooperation works well; no evidence supports scaling it to replace the state for complex modern societies.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-018
BELIEF: Democracy (one person, one vote, majority rule with minority protections) is the only legitimate form of government.
SYSTEMS: CL, SLP, SD, DS, LL, LP
PRESUPPOSITION: Political authority derives from the consent of the governed; no person's political judgment is inherently superior.
ASSESSABLE AS: "The only legitimate government is one where every adult has an equal vote and leaders are chosen by the people."
UNIQUE: No — broadly liberal-democratic position.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Democracy can be two wolves and a sheep voting on dinner; it produces mediocre leaders, short-term thinking, and tyranny of the majority. Other legitimate forms exist (constitutional monarchy, mixed constitutions, epistocracy). Systems: Mon, TC (qualified), TG, FNS.
RESEARCH: Democracies almost never wage war on each other ("democratic peace theory" — Doyle, 1983; Russett, 1993; supported by data from 1816-present). Democracies have higher GDP per capita on average (Acemoglu et al., 2019, using IV estimation). Sen ("Development as Freedom," 1999) showed no functioning democracy has experienced famine. Counter: Jason Brennan ("Against Democracy," 2016) documents voter ignorance — median U.S. voter cannot name their congressional representative. Singapore (semi-authoritarian) and China (authoritarian) have achieved rapid development without Western-style democracy, though Singapore is far wealthier per capita.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — democratic governance correlates with peace, prosperity, and famine prevention; whether it is the ONLY legitimate form is normative.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-019
BELIEF: Democracy is dangerous because majorities can tyrannize minorities; constitutional limits on democratic power are essential.
SYSTEMS: CL, RL, TC, NC, AC (partially)
PRESUPPOSITION: Crowds are irrational; pure majority rule leads to mob rule and rights violations.
ASSESSABLE AS: "There should be strict limits on what even a large majority can vote to do."
UNIQUE: No — broadly right-of-center and classical liberal.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Constitutional limits are themselves anti-democratic — they allow past generations to bind current ones and give unelected judges veto power over the people's will. Systems: LP, DS, ACom, AS.
RESEARCH: Madison's Federalist No. 10 (1788) articulated the tyranny-of-majority concern. Historical examples: Jim Crow laws were democratically enacted by white majorities; Weimar Germany's democratic collapse led to Nazi rule. The U.S. Bill of Rights, EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, and the ECHR all represent constitutional constraints on majority power. Counter: constitutional constraints can entrench minority tyranny — the U.S. Senate gives Wyoming (580K people) equal representation to California (39M), effectively a counter-majoritarian institution. Empirically, countries with stronger constitutional constraints tend to have more stable democracies but slower policy adaptation (Lijphart, "Patterns of Democracy," 2012).
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — historical evidence for both majority tyranny and the value of constitutional constraints is robust.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-020
BELIEF: A strong, centralized state is necessary to maintain order, defend the nation, and pursue national greatness.
SYSTEMS: FNS, NC, Nat, Mon, TG, RP (some)
PRESUPPOSITION: Without strong central authority, society fragments into chaos; strength and unity require centralized power.
ASSESSABLE AS: "A country needs a strong central government to maintain order and achieve greatness."
UNIQUE: No — shared across authoritarian and statist positions.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Centralized power is the single greatest threat to human freedom and prosperity; the 20th century's greatest atrocities were all committed by strong centralized states. Systems: RL, AC, LL, ACom, AS.
RESEARCH: The 20th century's most destructive regimes (Nazi Germany, Soviet Union, Maoist China, Khmer Rouge) were all strong centralized states — combined death toll exceeds 100 million (Rummel, "Death by Government," 1994). However, weak/failed states (Somalia, Libya post-2011, Yemen) also produce massive suffering. The optimal state strength appears to be moderate — strong enough to enforce law and provide services, constrained enough to protect rights. Singapore's strong centralized state has produced extraordinary prosperity and safety, complicating simple anti-centralization narratives. Acemoglu & Robinson ("The Narrow Corridor," 2019) argue that prosperity requires a balance between state power and societal power.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — both state weakness and state overreach produce catastrophic outcomes; the evidence supports moderate, constrained state strength.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-021
BELIEF: Political power should be radically decentralized to the most local level possible.
SYSTEMS: RL, AC, ACom, AS, LL, TC (partially — subsidiarity)
PRESUPPOSITION: Local communities understand their own needs better than distant authorities; centralization breeds corruption and incompetence.
ASSESSABLE AS: "Political decisions should be made at the most local level possible — neighborhood, city, or region rather than national government."
UNIQUE: No — shared across both anarchist and localist-conservative positions, though for different reasons.
COUNTER-BELIEF: Radical decentralization produces coordination failures, races to the bottom, and enables local tyrannies; some problems (climate, defense, trade) can only be solved at scale. Systems: NC, SLP, GI, SD.
RESEARCH: Switzerland's cantonal system is the closest large-democracy approximation of radical decentralization — high satisfaction, prosperity, and civic engagement (Frey & Stutzer, 2000). Catholic social teaching's subsidiarity principle (Quadragesimo Anno, 1931) provides the TC framework. Counter: the U.S. Articles of Confederation (1781-1789) failed due to excessive decentralization. Local control in the American South maintained segregation for a century after abolition — sometimes local preferences are unjust. Tiebout's (1956) model shows competition between localities can be efficient but assumes costless mobility, which is unrealistic for the poor.
CONFIDENCE: strong_signal — evidence supports significant devolution of power; radical decentralization has no successful large-scale example.

```
BELIEF ID: POL-022
BELIEF: Political authority should derive from divine mandate or religious law.
SYSTEMS: TG, Mon (divine right variant)

## NOTES FOR ASSESSMENT DESIGN

1. **Phrasing neutrality is critical.** Each "ASSESSABLE AS" phrasing avoids signaling which system it maps to. A respondent should be able to agree or disagree without feeling they're being placed into a political box.

2. **Overlapping beliefs are the most diagnostic.** A person who holds POL-001 (natural rights) AND POL-003 (negative rights only) AND POL-033 (taxation as theft) is very likely right-libertarian. But POL-001 alone could be classical liberal or left-libertarian.

3. **Presuppositions are the deepest layer.** Two people can agree on a policy position for entirely different reasons. The presupposition mapping allows assessment of WHY someone holds a belief, not just THAT they hold it.

4. **System assignment requires belief CLUSTERS, not individual beliefs.** No single belief maps cleanly to one system. The assessment should identify which cluster pattern best matches the respondent's profile.

5. **Many beliefs exist on spectrums, not as binaries.** Assessment should allow for degrees of agreement (strongly agree → strongly disagree) rather than yes/no.

6. **Some beliefs are in direct tension.** POL-003 vs. POL-004, POL-028 vs. POL-030, POL-048 vs. POL-050, etc. These tensions are diagnostic — how someone resolves them reveals their underlying framework.

7. **Counter-beliefs enable tension-pair assessment.** Each belief now has an explicit counter-belief, enabling paired assessment items that reveal which side of each tension a respondent occupies.

8. **Confidence ratings guide assessment interpretation.** Beliefs rated "irrefutable" should be treated differently from "hypothesis" — disagreement with irrefutable beliefs (e.g., that the death penalty deters crime) may indicate motivated reasoning or information gaps rather than genuine value differences.

9. **New systems (GP, Com, PC, TL) expand coverage.** Assessment should include items that distinguish Green Politics from generic progressivism, communitarianism from conservatism, and techno-libertarianism from traditional libertarianism.
